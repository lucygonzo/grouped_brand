DESIGN PRINCIPLE
Our dark is navy, not black.

Every dark surface carries a cool blue undertone that lets the warm bronze breathe. The temperature contrast between cool navy backgrounds and warm gold accents is what gives Grouped its depth. It's the same reason jewelers display gold on dark blue velvet, not black fabric.

Never use neutral greys (#1A1A1A, #222) in the dark theme. They read as dead spots that break the temperature relationship.

Exception: OLED mode. For mobile apps where battery matters, an OLED theme uses true black (#000000) with neutral white strokes instead of warm cream. This is a functional variant, not the brand default. The navy theme is always the primary dark experience.

The test: if the element is part of the brand voice (something you'd see on a poster), it's bronze. If the element is part of the interface (something you'd interact with or that tells you a current state), it's focus blue.

Card hover outlines use bronze, not focus blue. When an interactive card gets a hover state border, that border is --bronze-400. Focus blue (--focus) is reserved strictly for form input rings and keyboard navigation focus states. Bronze on a card hover communicates "this is something worth reaching for." Focus blue on a card would read like a form field.
Figma variable format: All opacity-based values in this system use decimals (0.07), not percentages (7%). Figma does not support percentage-based opacity in variables. When exporting JSON tokens for Storybook or Tailwind, verify every opacity-based surface and border color is in decimal format.

B1 / Philosophy

Make the screen disappear.

Every decision in this system serves one goal: make the digital experience feel physical. Tactile. Like an extension of your hands and ears. Music is not flat. It moves in cycles, in loops, in waves. The design should move the same way.

◌
CYCLICAL
Motion tells the next story.
Music lives in loops. Write, record, release, connect, repeat. Every animation should feel like it's returning to something, building on the last cycle. Rounded corners, circular motifs, spring easings that overshoot and settle. Nothing stops. Everything flows into the next beat.
◗
TACTILE
Surfaces you can almost touch.
Glass has weight. Cards tilt when you reach for them. Stickers resist then follow your hand. Grain gives every surface a texture you can feel through the screen. The experience should remind you of handling vinyl, flipping through a crate, holding something real.
◉
VIBING
The room has a tempo.
Timing is feel. Fast UI feels anxious. Slow UI feels confident. Our transitions run 500ms or longer because we're not in a hurry. We're vibing. The cursor glow drifts. Cards lift and settle with weight. Everything breathes at a pace that matches sitting in a listening room, not clicking through a dashboard.
THE FEELING
This should feel like an extension of the artist, not a tool they log into.
When an artist opens Grouped, the screen should dissolve. No chrome, no clutter, no "app" feeling. Just them, their music, and a warm room full of fans. The physical metaphors in this system (glass, grain, warmth, tilt, weight) exist to bridge the gap between pixels and the real relationship happening underneath. Fans should feel the same thing: not a platform, but a place.

DESIGN PRINCIPLE
Grouped is the room. Artists are the reason people walked in.
Every design decision should ask: does this elevate the artist's content, or compete with it? The deep blue recedes. The grain adds texture without noise. Glass surfaces frame without containing. When it works, artists feel like the platform was built around them. That is the goal.
INTERACTION LANGUAGE
Every shape and interaction borrows from music.

The recurring shapes across Grouped come from two places: the physics of sound and the hardware musicians use to control it. Flowing waveform strokes. Volume dials with clean tick marks. Frequency curves as progress indicators. These elements show up everywhere, from loading states to hover effects, because every interaction should remind you what this platform is for.

LIGHT ELEMENTS
Waveforms. Flowing lines. Frequency curves.

Thin, luminous, alive. These are the shapes that move and respond. Progress bars that ripple. Loading states that pulse. Hover trails that follow the cursor like sound moving through a room. Clean strokes, no gradients. Light on dark. Subtle and sexy until you notice them, then impossible to unsee.

SOLID ELEMENTS
Dials. Tick marks. Faders. Level meters.

Muted, structural, grounded. These shapes give the interface its weight. Volume dials with Swiss-minimal tick marks. Fader grooves as progress indicators. Level meters as data visualization. Flat fills, no shadows, no gradients. They feel like studio equipment because that is the vocabulary artists already think in.

THE INTERPLAY
Light shapes play against solid ones.

Light elements are thin strokes, frequency curves, luminous accents that respond and move. They catch your eye the way a waveform catches light on an oscilloscope. Solid elements are flat shapes with muted fills that hold position and communicate structure. The interplay gives the interface its character: precise but alive. Studio hardware with music running through it.

Real artists. Real energy.

Photography in Grouped is intimate, high-contrast, and warm. It shows artists in their element: performing, creating, connecting with fans. Every image should feel like a moment worth remembering.

High contrast, warm tones
Deep blacks, warm highlights. Color grade should complement the bronze accent. Slightly desaturated to keep the palette cohesive. Think film stock, not phone camera.
✓
Tight crops, real energy
Close-up and medium shots. Show hands on instruments, faces in crowds, studio sessions in progress. Intimacy over spectacle. Movement over posed stills.

Images blend into dark backgrounds naturally. Edge vignetting helps photos sit cleanly on dark surfaces without harsh borders or visible crop lines.
✓
Natural and ambient light
Stage lighting, studio lamps, golden hour. The light source should feel contextual, not manufactured. Embrace shadow and negative space within the frame.
✓
Diverse genres and settings
Grouped serves all artists. Photography should represent hip-hop producers, indie singer-songwriters, DJs, R&B vocalists, bands in rehearsal rooms. Varied, always authentic.
✓
Fan perspective shots
Include the fan side: crowd shots from the artist's POV, phones held up at shows, merch tables, listening sessions. This is a two-sided platform. Show both sides.
✕
No stock photography
No generic studio portraits with flat lighting. No "musician smiling at camera" stock imagery. If we don't have the right photo, use the ring motif or type treatment instead.
✕
No white/light device frames
Product mockups always use dark device frames on dark backgrounds. White device frames break the visual language. Screenshots should reflect the app's native dark mode.
✕
No oversaturated or filtered images
No Instagram-style presets, no VSCO film emulations, no heavy color grading that distorts skin tones. Warmth comes from lighting and subtle post, not from filters layered on top.
Post-Production Specs
Color Temperature
Warm bias. Push toward 5500-6500K. Shadows lean slightly warm (amber/brown), highlights lean neutral.
Saturation
Pull back 10-20% from natural. Let the bronze accent be the warmest thing in any composition. Skin tones stay natural.
Contrast
High. Lift blacks slightly (to around 5-10%) so shadows have detail but stay rich. Highlights can blow out in stage lighting contexts.
Grain
Optional. Subtle film grain (ISO 400-800 equivalent) adds texture for editorial contexts. Skip grain in product screenshots and clean UI shots.

B8 / Brand Elements & Textures

The physical toolkit.

These are the graphic elements that make Grouped feel like Grouped. Concentric arcs from the logo DNA. Physical controls borrowed from studio hardware. Badge treatments pulled from 70s patch culture. Every element reinforces the tactile philosophy: this is something you can almost reach out and touch.

THE SIGNATURE MOTIF
Concentric arcs.

Sound waves radiating from a source. Vinyl grooves. Ripples. The concentric arc is the single most recognizable Grouped graphic element. Heavy strokes with tight gaps between them. The stroke is always wider than the gap (roughly 2.5:1). No feathering, no gradients, no per-ring opacity variation. Solid lines doing structural work.

ARC RULE
Heavy strokes. Tight gaps. Stroke width is always larger than the gap between arcs (roughly 2.5:1 ratio). Every ring in a set uses the same weight and color. No opacity variation between individual strokes. When used as background, reduce the group opacity, not individual rings.
PHYSICAL CONTROLS
Studio hardware as UI.

Controls borrowed from studio hardware, stripped to their structural essence. No gradients, no metallic effects, no embossing. The shape itself communicates the function. Circles rotate. TEXTURE PRINCIPLE
Flat controls. Warm room. No contradiction.
Controls are minimal and structural. The room around them (glass surfaces, grain texture, ambient glow) provides the warmth. This contrast is intentional: crisp UI elements sitting inside a warm, textured environment. The only gradients that exist are the ones mimicking warm studio light on surfaces, never on the controls themselves.
REMEMBER
Every pixel should vibe.
If something feels flat, add texture. If something feels sharp, round it. If something feels fast, slow it down. If something feels static, let it breathe. The test is always the same: does this feel like a place you'd want to sit and listen to music in?

✓ Do

Use Destructive only for irreversible actions (delete, remove)

✗ Don't
Don't stack two Primary buttons — demote one to Secondary
Don't use CTA for admin/artist actions — use Secondary
Don't use Destructive for cancel or dismiss — use Secondary
Don't mix button sizes in the same row

P10 / Responsive Breakpoints

Responsive breakpoints.

Four breakpoint ranges, mobile-first. Build for the smallest screen first, add complexity at each step up. Minimum 44px touch targets at all sizes.

Mobile
0 — 639px
Single column. Fan-facing swap pages, mobile web app. Stacked layouts, full-width CTAs.
Tablet
640 — 1023px
Two columns where useful. Sidebar collapses to hamburger. Card grids go 2-up.
Desktop
1024 — 1439px
Primary artist dashboard experience. Sidebar always visible. Full data tables and charts.
Wide
1440px+
Max container width 1280px. Extra space becomes margins. Analytics and data-dense views expand.
TOUCH TARGETS
44
44px minimum
Primary actions: buttons, nav items, CTA areas
36
36px minimum
Secondary actions: icon buttons, table rows, filter chips
RESPONSIVE ADJUSTMENTS
Type Scale
D0 caps at 48px on mobile (from 96px). D1 caps at 28px. Body stays 14px everywhere. Component type (C1-C7) unchanged.
Spacing
Reduce to 75% of desktop values on mobile for space-8 and above. Small spacings (space-1 through space-6) stay the same.
Layout
Sidebar collapses at 1024px. Card grids collapse from 2-col to 1-col at 640px. Tables scroll horizontally on mobile.
CONFIRMED FOR LAUNCH
  • Two breakpoints: mobile narrow and desktop.
  • Design standard width: 1440px. Past 1440px, outer margins and gutters scale; content holds max-width.
  • Column grid: 3-column layout for main content area on desktop, stacks to single column on mobile.
